Blink an LED with ESP32

Your first mission is the classic maker milestone — make a light blink! By the end, you'll know how ESP32 talks to the outside world through its pins.

Mission 1 Beginner 12 min mission
💡 Mission 1

Make Your First Light Blink!

Imagine you're the captain of a tiny spaceship — the ESP32. Right now your ship is quiet. No blinking lights, no signs of life. Mission Control wants proof that you're online.

Today you send your first command: turn on a small LED. When it blinks, you'll know your ESP32 heard you, your wiring works, and you're officially a maker.

Explain Like I'm 12

An LED is a tiny light. Your ESP32 is like a brain with wires called pins. When the brain sends power to a pin, electricity flows through the LED and it glows. Turn power off — light goes dark. Blink on, blink off — that's your first program!

What You'll Build

A breadboard circuit with one LED connected to your ESP32. You'll upload a short program that blinks the LED every half second — proof that your board, USB cable, and code all work together.

Things You'll Need

  • ESP32 DevKit board — Any board with USB port
  • LED — Any color — 5 mm is perfect
  • 220 Ω resistor — Red-Red-Brown bands
  • Breadboard — Half-size is fine
  • Jumper wires — Male-to-male, at least 3
  • USB cable — Data-capable, not charge-only
⚠️Safety First
  • Use 3.3 V logic only — do not connect 5 V directly to ESP32 GPIO pins.
  • Ask an adult to help if you're unsure about plugging in USB or handling small parts.
  • Disconnect power before changing wiring.

How Does Blinking Work?

GPIO means General Purpose Input/Output — a pin the ESP32 can turn HIGH (3.3 V) or LOW (0 V). Your LED needs a resistor so too much current doesn't burn it out.

In code, pinMode sets the pin as output. digitalWrite(HIGH) turns the LED on. delay waits. digitalWrite(LOW) turns it off. Repeat — blink!

Wiring

  1. Unplug USB. Place ESP32 and breadboard side by side.
  2. Insert the LED — long leg (anode) in one row, short leg (cathode) in another.
  3. Connect 220 Ω resistor from ESP32 GPIO2 to the LED anode row.
  4. Connect LED cathode row to ESP32 GND with a jumper wire.
  5. Double-check: resistor between GPIO2 and anode, cathode to GND.
  6. Plug in USB. Many DevKit boards also have a built-in LED on GPIO2.

Code

Copy this into Arduino IDE, then click Upload.

blink_led.ino 
#define LED_PIN 2

void setup() {
  pinMode(LED_PIN, OUTPUT);
}

void loop() {
  digitalWrite(LED_PIN, HIGH);
  delay(500);
  digitalWrite(LED_PIN, LOW);
  delay(500);
}

Expected Output

The LED blinks on for half a second, off for half a second, repeating forever. On many DevKit boards you'll see the same blink on the tiny onboard LED near the USB port.

If nothing happens, check that GPIO2 matches your wire and that the LED long leg faces the resistor.

Challenge Yourself

Change the blink speed — try delay(200) for a fast blink or delay(1000) for a slow pulse. Can you make the LED blink a SOS pattern (three short, three long, three short)?
